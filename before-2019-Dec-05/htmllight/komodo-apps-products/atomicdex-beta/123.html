Instead, use a mechanism that combines everyone's liquidity and creates markets in accordance with a specific algorithm. This algorithm, known as the automated market maker (AMM), sets prices for the user according to some predefined set of rules. An example of a very simple AMM is a bot strategy, which puts out buy and sell orders exactly one dollar respectively lower and higher than the average market price and constantly reviews order placement as the market moves.

Just give an example, an example using the KMD / KmdUSD trading pair. Let's assume that market makers co-financed a pool of 100,000 KmdUSD and 1,000 KMD. DEX takes these two values and multiplies them (100,000 x 1000 = 100,000,000). initial conditions: KmdUSD liquidity KMD liquidity product                                      100,000 1,000 100,000,000                                            x y k
The purpose of DEX for this particular trading pair is to preserve the result, i.e. multiplying these two values, equal to 100 million, regardless of how many trading operations take place. The key formula to keep in mind is x * y = k, where x and y are the number of coins in the liquidity pool and k is the result. To keep the constant k, x and y must be inversely dependent, i.e. if x goes up, then y goes down, and vice versa. When a trader makes a KMD purchase under this contract, he increases x (since they add KmdUSD to the liquidity pool) and decreases y (when they remove KMDUSD from the liquidity pool). But this scheme does not scale linearly. Trying to buy 100 KMD instead of 10 KMD does not just require 10 times more KmdUSD. In fact, it increases asymptotically.
